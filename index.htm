In a moment Reader is going to read Psalm 139. Before she does, I want to tell you what to listen for.

Over recent weeks we've looked at hope in some of life's biggest moments — anxiety, illness, eternity, death. Intense, urgent, threshold moments. This psalm feels different from many of David's. He's not on a battlefield. He's not in crisis. He's just going about his day — and being struck, in the middle of it, by how close God actually is.

That's where we're going this morning.

It moves through four stages, and it's worth knowing where it's going.

· · ·

God is close. Ordinarily, inescapably close — in the sitting and the rising, the going out and the lying down. Much like what we just heard from our three contributors.

God is always close. Before birth, before a single day was lived, before anyone else even knew we existed.

David pours out his heart. Not the tidy version — the real one. The anger, the fear, the people he can't stop thinking about. Verses 19 to 22 are uncomfortable reading, and I don't want us to skip past them. They're there because David doesn't hide what he really feels in front of God. He brings the whole thing.

David asks God to search his heart. Search me. Know my heart. See if there's anything in me that needs changing. Lead me.

· · ·

It's not a resolved prayer. It's an honest one.

Part 2 · Psalm 139 · 12 minutes

Opening — callback, then defining hope

A little earlier, our three contributors told us about the small, ordinary things that keep them rooted in God. Nothing dramatic. Nothing you'd put in a highlight reel. Just small, repeated things, most days. And then we heard the psalm that's been doing that same thing for three thousand years.

I want to spend a few minutes on why that ordinary, repeated rhythm matters more than we tend to think.

But first — a quick word on what we mean by "hope." It isn't wishful thinking — hoping it doesn't rain, hoping the traffic isn't bad. Biblical hope is something firmer than that. It's not based on how we feel. It's based on who God is, and what he's already done. Paul calls it hope that doesn't put us to shame, because it's anchored in God's love — not our circumstances. It's solid ground. Not crossed fingers.

Series recap and thesis

Over the past few weeks we've been working through a series on hope, built around our verse of the year, Romans 15:13. We've looked at hope in the face of uncertainty, trouble, eternity, and death. Four big moments.

And here's what struck me, looking at that list: every one of those is exceptional. They're the moments that interrupt ordinary life. None of us gets a calendar invite for a crisis. So here's the question I've been sitting with: what actually builds people — what builds a church — that can hold hope out to each other when one of those moments arrives? I don't think it gets built in the moment itself. I think it gets built slowly. In all the ordinary weeks in between.

· · ·

So this morning isn't about doing more. It's about noticing that the quiet, repeated, unremarkable rhythm of an ordinary Wednesday is already doing more than we give it credit for.

Move 1 — known in the ordinary

Psalm 139 is a good place to start, because it reads differently from David's crisis psalms — the ones written from battlefields, hospital beds, gravesides. David knew all three. As far as we can tell, the psalmist is just going about his day. And in the middle of that ordinary day — much like the ones we heard about earlier — he's struck by something: he is known. Completely. Before he sits down. Before he says a word. Before he's gone anywhere. God already knows.

The psalm opens with this — you have searched me, you know when I sit and when I rise, you know my thoughts from a distance, and before a word is even on my tongue, you know it completely.

· · ·

That reads to me as an ordinary-day prayer, not a crisis prayer. And that's exactly the point. If hope is solid ground rather than wishful thinking, this is where the ground gets laid — not in the dramatic moment, but in being known on a day when nothing much is happening. The psalm doesn't argue this directly — but look at what it shows us.

What our three contributors told us this morning shows there are many different ways into this. Different rhythms, different habits, different shapes — and every one of them has kept them closer to what we just heard in that psalm. For others it might look different again — something older, like morning and evening prayer, the rhythm Christians have leaned on for centuries. None of it prescriptive. We do what works for us, because we want to draw closer to God. It's not about the method.

· · ·

It's about staying within reach of the fact that you're known, however that happens for you.

Move 2 — the Timothy story

Tell this conversationally — don't read it. Let the surprise and the laughter land before the turn.

Let me tell you something that happened in our family recently.

We were expecting a grandchild. We were told it was a boy. We were confident enough that we'd already picked a name — Timothy. We talked about Timothy for months. Pictured Timothy. And then Timothy arrived...

· · ·

and Timothy was a girl.

Let the laughter land. Do not talk over it. Wait it out fully.

Fairly minor mix-up, in the scheme of things — a scan, a guess, an assumption that didn't hold. But it got me thinking about verses 13 to 16 of that same psalm:

You formed my inward parts,
you knitted me together in my mother's womb.
My frame wasn't hidden from you
when I was being formed in secret.
All my days were written in your book
before a single one of them came to be. Psalm 139:13–16

We didn't know whether our grandchild was a boy or a girl until she arrived. The hospital didn't fully know either. But God knew. Not guessed — knew. Before the scan, before the name, before any of our expectations were even formed, she was already known completely, every day of her life already written down. Our certainty was wrong. His knowledge never was.

· · ·
One short sentence at a time — give each one a beat.

He knew her before any of us did. I think that means he's not waiting for us to get it right before he shows up, either. He's already there — in the ordinary Wednesdays, the same as he was with her, before anyone else even knew.

· · ·

That's what ordinary-time hope rests on. Not our predictions, which we get wrong all the time. Just the plain fact that we're known, named, and held — by someone whose knowledge of us never needed correcting.

Move 2b — the honest turn (vv.19–22)

But as we heard — the psalm doesn't stay in that reassuring place. Verses 19 to 22 take a sharp turn. The anger, the fear, the enemies David can't stop thinking about. He doesn't tidy it away. He brings the whole thing.

· · ·

And that's part of what ordinary faithfulness looks like too. Not just the gratitude and the quiet moments. All of it. You are still searched and known in the middle of all of that. Verse 23 brings the psalm back to exactly that place — search me, God, and know my heart.

· · ·

Not tidy. Not resolved. But known. Still held.

Move 3 — bringing it together

There's something worth saying about a garden, even without scripture to back it up. Nobody stands over a flowerbed every single day demanding to see something happen. Most of the work a plant does, it does where you can't see it — underground, before there's anything to show for it.

· · ·

Long before a single flower appears, the roots are already going down, quietly, day after unremarkable day. By the time you see the flower, the real work's long since been done, out of sight.

That's what ordinary, daily faithfulness with God is like. We're more certain of his hope for the moments we're called to encourage someone else, precisely because of all those quiet, unremarkable days that came before — the days where nothing visible was happening, but the roots were already going down. That certainty doesn't get manufactured in a crisis.

· · ·

It's grown slowly, out of all the Wednesdays where nothing much seemed to be happening at all.

Direct eye contact with the three speakers here, if possible.

Think back to our three contributors this morning. Nothing dramatic in what they told us. That's rather the point. There's no single right pattern here. What works for you, works for you. And if you miss a day — you will — don't beat yourself up about it. Go back to it tomorrow.

· · ·

A plant survives a missed watering. What matters is going back to it.

If you want a small nudge for the week ahead: try a hymn you've never sat with properly, or open a book of the Bible you've never spent time in. But don't mistake that for the point. The point isn't novelty.

· · ·

The point is that the rhythm you already have — however small — is already doing its work, mostly out of sight.

Closing prayer

Lord, you have searched us and known us, in our ordinary days as much as our extraordinary ones. Thank you that we are known before a word is on our tongue, with every day already written in your book. Teach us to bring all of it to you — the gratitude and the fear, the quiet moments and the things we'd rather not name. And as we tend the ordinary rhythm of our days, grow roots in us that run deeper than we can see, so that when eternity presses close — for us, or for someone beside us — we've got hope enough to hold out to one another. Search us, know our hearts, and lead us. In Jesus' name, Amen.
